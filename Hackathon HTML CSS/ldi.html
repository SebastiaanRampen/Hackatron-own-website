Long chain Diol Index (LDI)

Organisms adjust their lipid composition as an adaptation mechanism to changes in environmental conditions such as temperature, in order to maintain the right fluidity of their cell membranes (1, 2). If such lipids are exclusively produced by organisms that show the same correlation between their lipid composition and temperature, and if these lipids remain preserved in the sediments, then these lipids may be applicable for climate reconstruction purposes.

In a comprehensive study on long-chain diol distributions in marine sediments, it was observed that long-chain 1,13-diols predominantly occurred in areas with low water temperatures, whereas in warm areas, C30 1,15-diol was relatively more abundant. The long chain Diol Index (LDI) was introduced, in which C30 1,15-diol abundance was expressed relative to those of C28 1,13-, C30 1,13- and C30 1,15-diols, and this index showed a strong correlation with the annual mean sea surface temperatures of the overlying waters (3).

As a result, by analyzing the long chain diol composition of ancient sea sediments calculating the LDI value, the annual mean sea surface temperature can be reconstructed from the time the sediments were formed (Fig. 2). Long chain diols have been reported in sediments up to the Cretaceous (4), suggesting the LDI can provide an important role in climate reconstruction studies.
