C32 1,15-diols and freshwater input

In the marine environment, long chain 1,13- and 1,15-diols show a strong correlation with sea surface temperature (1), but the correlation between long chain diol distributions and temperature is not as clear in freshwater environments (2). Possibly, marine long chain 1,13- and 1,15-diols are mainly derived from one closely related group of eustigmatophytes which show similar correlations between growth temperature and their long chain diol distribution, whereas multiple sources, with each their own long-chain diol distributions occur in freshwater environments (1, 2, Rampen et al., under review).

Almost all eustigmatophyte cultures that have been analyzed so far contained relative C32 1,15-diol abundances that are higher than observed in most marine environments. In addition, most of these cultures contained mono-unsaturated C32:1 1,15-diol, which is rarely observed in the natural environment. Possibly, the most dominant species in the natural environment have not been isolated yet (3; see fig. 2).

The long chain diol index (LDI) does not seem to correlate with temperature in freshwater environments or marine areas that are affected by freshwater input (1, 2, 4). C32 1,15-diol is often relatively abundant in marine environments with freshwater input, and therefore, the relative abundance of C32 1,15-diol may be an indication of freshwater input (4, 5). Indeed, C32 1,15-diol concentrations relative to the combined 1,13- and 1,15-diol abundances from a sediment core from the Murray Canyons area offshore southeastern Australia, covering the last 135 ka (6), seem to be a good indicator of river-input (fig. 3). Enhanced abundances of C32 1,15-diol may be a good indication that standard LDI values do not correlate well with sea surface temperatures, and possibly, C32 1,15-diol concentrations may be used to correct for the freshwater effect (Rampen et al., under review).
