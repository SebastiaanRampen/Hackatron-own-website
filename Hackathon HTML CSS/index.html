Introduction: Long chain diols in aquatic environments

Long chain diols LCDs) are lipid molecules consisting of straight-chain n-alkanes and alcohol groups at the first carbon position and at a position in the center of the chain (fig. 1). After their first discovery in Black Sea sediments by De Leeuw et al. (1), it has become clear they are widely distributed in both marine and freshwater environments (2–4).

For a long time, it remained unclear which organisms produced these lipids. Volkman et al. reported the presence of long chain 1,13- and 1,15-diols in both marine and freshwater eustigmatophytes (5, 6), and combined lipid and DNA analyses confirmed that these algae are a likely source of LCDs in freshwater environments (7). However, eustigmatophytes are rarely reported in marine environments, and in a combined lipid and DNA study in the western North Atlantic Ocean, LCDs were commonly identified whereas eustigmatophyte algae were nearly absent (8). Only recently, a new study of combined lipid and DNA analyses and thorough examination of previously published data revealed that marine LCDs are most likely produced by a novel clade of eustigmatophytes (Rampen et al., under review).

In addition to long chain 1,13- and 1,15-diols, there are reports of long chain 1,14-diols which are often found in nutrient-rich environments. These lipids have been reported in the marine diatom species Proboscia (9, 10), which are often found in upwelling areas (11). Moreover, long chain 1,14-diols have been reported in the species Apedinella radians (12), although the role of this species as a major long chain diol source is unlikely.

Several climate proxies are based on long chain diol distributions. The abundance of long chain 1,14-diols relative to long chain 1,13- or 1,15-diols has been used as a proxy for upwelling intensity or nutrient availability (15, 16), the relative abundance of C32 1,15-diol has been used as an indicator for freshwater input in marine environments (4, 17), and the ratio between C30 1,15-diol and C28 and C30 1,13-diol has been used as a proxy for surface water temperature (3). However, further research is still needed to constrain and validate these proxies.
